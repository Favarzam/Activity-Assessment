# Representative SIOP Case Analysis

## Purpose

This memo adds a representative-case analysis to the prior SIOP analysis of the 35 SciLing Column C activities. The purpose is to select one activity that best represents the SIOP components and features, then use that activity as a qualitative sample for explaining how SciLing-generated activity text can reflect SIOP, the 5E model, and translanguaging-oriented multilingual learner support.

## Recommended Placement in the Dissertation Paper

The full activity text should not appear first in the findings section. The activity is long, and beginning with the complete raw artifact would delay the interpretation. Instead, the findings section should present a brief case-selection rationale, a compact activity vignette, and targeted excerpts connected directly to SIOP, 5E, and translanguaging claims. The full Activity 13 text should be placed in an appendix and referenced from the findings section.

Suggested dissertation sentence:

Activity 13 was selected as a representative qualitative case because it showed the strongest written SIOP alignment across the 35 SciLing activities. The main findings section presents a vignette and targeted excerpts tied to SIOP, 5E, and translanguaging constructs; the full generated activity text is provided in Appendix A for transparency.

## Activity Vignette for the Main Text

Activity 13 is a kindergarten physical science lesson on comparing pushes and pulls. The generated activity includes both a content objective and a language objective: students describe different pushes and pulls using classroom objects or simple machines, and they use sentence frames to compare and describe those actions in oral discussion. The lesson is organized as a short hands-on sequence: students explore objects in mixed-proficiency groups, discuss and chart which objects are pushed, pulled, or both, and then complete a quick formative reflection using examples, picture cards, or thumbs-up/thumbs-down checks. The activity also includes an extension in which students solve a movement challenge with tools such as string, rulers, toy cars, or scarves.

Activity excerpt: "Objective: Content: Students will describe different types of pushes and pulls using classroom objects or simple machines. Language: Students will use sentence frames to compare and describe pushes and pulls in oral discussion."

The lesson includes explicit multilingual learner supports: gestures, visuals, real objects, sentence frames, mixed-proficiency grouping, bilingual labels or picture cards, and opportunities to use home language before sharing in English. It also includes California standards alignment for both science and ELA. The main lesson cites NGSS K-PS2-1 and CA CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.SL.K.1; the extension cites NGSS K-PS2-2 and CA CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.SL.K.4.

Activity excerpt: "Translanguaging Strategies Allow students to discuss findings in their home language before sharing in English. Use bilingual labels or picture cards. Invite students to share how 'push' and 'pull' are said in their home language."

## Starting Point: Teacher's Initial Idea

Before the AI conversations, the teacher's initial activity idea already contained the core instructional structure: a kindergarten physical science lesson on comparing pushes and pulls, a measurable objective, group exploration with machines or objects, whole-class discussion/charting, informal assessment, pairing native English speakers with non-native English speakers, and sentence frames for discussion. This starting point matters because SciLing did not create the activity from nothing; it expanded and structured an existing teacher idea.

Teacher initial idea excerpt: "By the end of the lesson my students will be able to describe different types of pushes and pulls with 90% accuracy. My students will work in groups to explore different machines or objects that have push or pull mechanisms and discuss with their group the difference."

The teacher's original plan was already aligned with several SIOP-relevant practices: a clear objective, peer interaction, language modeling, sentence frames, and informal assessment. However, it was less developed in its sequencing, differentiation, explicit multilingual supports, state standards alignment, and assessment detail. In this sense, the initial idea functions as the seed of the final activity, while the AI interactions show how that seed became more fully elaborated.

Teacher initial idea excerpt: "The teacher will pair Native English speakers with non Native English speakers to serve as language models. The teacher will provide sentence frames when eliciting discussion from the group."

## Central Finding

Activity 13 illustrates how SciLing can generate a lesson design that reflects the SIOP model as a whole rather than isolated language supports. Its strength is that content objectives, language objectives, hands-on investigation, scaffolds, interaction, review, assessment, and standards alignment are integrated into a coherent lesson sequence. The activity is also a strong 5E case because students engage with familiar objects, explore pushes and pulls through manipulation, explain findings through discussion and charting, elaborate through a problem-solving extension, and evaluate understanding through quick checks and teacher notes. Translanguaging appears as a bridge for multilingual learners, especially through home-language discussion, bilingual labels, and labeling/drawing in English and/or home language.

Activity excerpt: "Use hands-on investigation to anchor science concepts in real experience. Scaffold language through sentence frames, visuals, and peer modeling. Encourage all students to participate in group and whole-class discussions, using home language as a bridge when needed. Integrate reading, speaking, listening, and writing (drawing/labeling) to reinforce both science and language objectives."

## After the SciLing Result: PT-Bot Refinement

After the SciLing-generated activity, the teacher's later conversation with the PT bot refined Activity 13 without abandoning its core design. The PT-refined version kept the same standards, the same science focus, and the same measurable objective, but made the lesson more concrete, playful, and instructionally responsive. It added pre-lesson family participation, expanded the hands-on exploration into rotating stations, named the "both" category more explicitly, assigned student roles, expanded sentence frames, and replaced or supplemented the original extension with a schoolwide "Force Walk."

PT-refined activity excerpt: "Standards (unchanged): NGSS K-PS2-1; CA CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.SL.K.1. Objective: Students will describe whether familiar objects can be pushed, pulled, or both, with 90% accuracy, using sentence frames in oral discussion."

This refinement is meaningful because it strengthens the original SIOP and 5E affordances that were already visible in the SciLing output. The pre-lesson bilingual note extends background building into the home and invites family/community knowledge into the science lesson. The three rotating stations make the Explore phase more varied and concrete. The "Both bucket" addresses a possible conceptual confusion by making "both" a legitimate category rather than forcing students to choose only push or pull. Student roles such as Reporter, Demonstrator, and Vocabulary Helper also reduce participation imbalance and make oral language practice more accountable.

PT-refined activity excerpt: "Set up three rotating stations. Mixed-proficiency groups rotate through each for ~2.5 min." The stations include familiar objects, a "Mystery pulley," and a "Both bucket" beside "PUSH" and "PULL" buckets.

The PT-bot refinement also strengthens translanguaging and culturally responsive connections. The home-sourced object task invites students to bring an object or drawing from home, creating a bridge between household experience and classroom science. The evaluation prompt, "I brought a ___. We ___ it at home," positions students' everyday practices as valid evidence for science talk. This is a more explicit home-school connection than the SciLing version, which mainly allowed home-language use during group discussion and labeling.

PT-refined activity excerpt: "Send home a bilingual note: 'Help your child find one object at home that pushes, pulls, or both. Bring it (or a drawing of it) to school on Tuesday.'" The evaluation asks students to share home objects: "I brought a ___. We ___ it at home."

The refined version also makes the extension more embodied and comparative. Instead of only solving a movement problem in the classroom, students take a "Force Walk" through the playground, hallway, and lunchroom, sketching or photographing one push, one pull, and one "both" from each location. This broadens the Elaborate phase by asking students to compare forces across contexts and then return to class to discuss patterns in what they observed.

PT-refined activity excerpt: "Take clipboards on a walk through playground, hallway, and lunchroom. Students sketch or photograph one push, one pull, and one 'both' from each location. Compare back in class: 'We saw pushes on the playground. We saw pulls in the lunchroom.'"

| SciLing-generated activity | Expanded the idea into a full SIOP- and 5E-aligned lesson with objectives, materials, timing, differentiated language supports, translanguaging moves, standards, and extension. | The activity became a coherent written lesson plan with strong evidence across SIOP components and 5E phases. |
| PT-bot refined activity | Made the activity more playful, concrete, and responsive by adding home-sourced objects, rotating stations, a mystery pulley, a "both" category, student roles, and a Force Walk. | The refinement strengthened engagement, conceptual clarity, participation equity, home-school connection, and cross-location application. |

This development sequence can be presented in the dissertation as evidence that the AI-supported design process was iterative rather than one-step generation. The teacher began with a sound but compact activity idea. SciLing elaborated that idea into a strong SIOP/5E/translanguaging-aligned written activity. The PT bot then refined the activity further by adding possibility-thinking elements, addressing potential classroom risks, and making the final design more actionable for young learners.

## SIOP Component Analysis

### Lesson Preparation

The activity includes both a content objective and a language objective. The content objective asks students to describe pushes and pulls with classroom objects or simple machines. The language objective asks students to use sentence frames to compare and describe pushes and pulls in oral discussion. This pairing reflects SIOP's emphasis on planning for both disciplinary content and academic language. The lesson is also developmentally appropriate for kindergarten because the science concepts are anchored in familiar actions and concrete objects such as toy cars, doors, drawers, and wind-up toys.

Activity excerpt: "Materials: Objects with push/pull mechanisms (toy cars, doors, drawers, wind-up toys, etc.) Chart paper or whiteboard Markers."

### Building Background

The activity links new concepts to experiences that young children already know: pushing a car, pulling a drawer, opening a door, moving a basket, or using classroom objects. These everyday examples make the abstract distinction between force types visible and physically meaningful. Vocabulary is also introduced through repetition, visuals, gestures, and sentence frames, especially the key terms "push," "pull," "move," and "stop."

Activity excerpt: "Students investigate: 'How do you make this object move? Do you push it, pull it, or both?'" The extension also connects to familiar contexts: "Quick 'Simon Says' game: 'Push your chair,' 'Pull your backpack,' etc."

### Comprehensible Input

The activity provides multiple techniques for making input understandable: real objects, visual charting, drawings or photos, gestures, simple questions, modeling, and proficiency-specific sentence frames. Beginning students can point, gesture, or answer yes/no and either/or questions. Intermediate students receive expanded frames, while advanced students are invited to compare objects and explain why some can be both pushed and pulled. This shows SIOP's comprehensible input principle because meaning is not carried by oral explanation alone.

Activity excerpt: "Use visuals (drawings or photos) to support understanding. Elicit student responses using sentence frames." The activity also states: "Allow students to respond with gestures or by pointing."

### Strategies

The lesson uses scaffolding as an instructional design principle rather than as an add-on. Students investigate, discuss, compare, draw, label, and explain. The extension activity adds problem solving by asking students how to move a heavier object from one side of the room to another. This extends the task beyond naming examples and moves students toward strategic thinking about force and motion.

Activity excerpt: "Present a simple challenge: 'How can we move a heavy object (e.g., a box of books, a basket of blocks) from one side of the room to the other?'" The extension then asks students to "brainstorm and test different ways to move the object using pushes, pulls, or both."

### Interaction

The activity repeatedly creates student-to-student and student-to-teacher interaction. Students work in mixed-proficiency groups, talk with peers during object exploration, contribute to whole-class charts, and share examples during reflection. The activity also intentionally allows home-language discussion before English sharing, which supports SIOP Feature 19 on clarification in the first language. The main missing SIOP feature is Feature 18, wait time, because the written text does not explicitly state that students receive extended thinking time before responding.

Activity excerpt: "Place students in mixed-proficiency groups. Each group receives 2-3 objects." It also states, "Encourage students to try out the objects and talk with their group using gestures and simple language."

### Practice and Application

Practice is hands-on and language-rich. Students manipulate objects, test pushes and pulls, describe what they observe, use oral sentence frames, participate in charting, and draw or label observations. The activity therefore connects content practice with speaking, listening, writing, drawing, and emergent reading of labels/sentence frames. The practice is not just a worksheet after instruction; it is the core of the lesson.

Activity excerpt: The activity asks students to use frames such as "I push the ____," "I pull the ____," and "This moves when I ____ it."

### Lesson Delivery

The written sequence is tightly aligned with the stated objectives. The activity begins with exploration, moves to explanation and discussion, and ends with reflection and assessment. The pacing is explicit and age-appropriate: 5 minutes for exploration, 5 minutes for discussion, and 3-5 minutes for evaluation/reflection. Because students are actively doing, talking, pointing, drawing, and sharing throughout the lesson, the design supports high engagement.

Activity excerpt: "Step-by-Step Activity 1. Engage & Explore (5 minutes) ... 2. Explain & Discuss (5 minutes) ... 3. Evaluate & Reflect (3-5 minutes)."

### Review and Assessment

Review and assessment are built into the lesson rather than postponed to the end only. Students review vocabulary with visuals and gestures, share one example of a push and one example of a pull, and complete quick checks such as thumbs-up/thumbs-down or picture cards. The teacher notes both language use and content understanding. This makes assessment formative and tied to the content and language objectives.

Activity excerpt: "Ask students to share one example of a push and one of a pull. Use a quick thumbs-up/thumbs-down or picture card check for understanding. Teacher notes language use and understanding for informal assessment."

## 5E Alignment

| 5E element | How Activity 13 shows it | Activity excerpt |
|---|---|---|
| Engage | Students begin with familiar objects and the guiding question of how to make the objects move. | "Students investigate: 'How do you make this object move? Do you push it, pull it, or both?'" |
| Explore | Small groups physically manipulate objects and test whether they push, pull, or do both. | "Each group receives 2-3 objects... Encourage students to try out the objects and talk with their group using gestures and simple language." |
| Explain | Students discuss findings, contribute to a class chart, and use sentence frames to describe the relationship between action and motion. | "Bring the class together. Chart findings: 'Which objects do we push? Which do we pull? Which can be both?'... Elicit student responses using sentence frames." |
| Elaborate | The extension asks students to solve a movement challenge using tools such as string, rulers, toy cars, or scarves. | "Present a simple challenge: 'How can we move a heavy object... from one side of the room to the other?'" |
| Evaluate | Students share examples, respond with quick visual checks, draw or label observations, and the teacher records evidence of content and language understanding. | "Use a quick thumbs-up/thumbs-down or picture card check for understanding. Teacher notes language use and understanding for informal assessment." |

The 5E alignment is especially strong because the phases are not only labeled. The functions of the phases are visible in the student actions. Students are invited to experience the phenomenon, name and discuss it, apply it in a new problem-solving context, and show understanding through multiple forms of evidence.

Activity excerpt: "Review & Warm-Up (3 minutes): Quick recap and vocabulary review. Challenge & Investigation (7 minutes): Hands-on problem-solving in groups. Share & Reflect (5 minutes): Group presentations and class discussion."

## Translanguaging Interpretation

The translanguaging evidence in Activity 13 is explicit, but it is not as saturated as the SIOP and 5E evidence. This is why Activity 13 is best described as a strong SIOP representative with moderate translanguaging integration.

The activity supports home-language sense-making by allowing students to discuss findings in their home language before sharing in English. It supports cross-language bridging by inviting students to share how "push" and "pull" are said in their home language and then connect those terms to English sentence frames. It supports multilingual artifacts by allowing students to write or draw observations and label them in English and/or their home language. It also uses bilingual labels or picture cards as vocabulary supports.

Activity excerpt: "Allow students to use home language to discuss in groups, then share in English." The activity also states: "Allow students to write or draw their observations and label them in English and/or their home language."

Qualitatively, this matters because home language is treated as a resource for science reasoning, not as something separate from the lesson. Students can first use their full linguistic repertoire to make sense of motion, then move toward English academic expression through sentence frames, shared vocabulary, labels, and whole-class discussion.

At the same time, some translanguaging dimensions remain less developed. The written activity provides only limited evidence of bilingual instructional resources, flexible multilingual assessment, family/community language resources, or systematic assessment of bilingual products. Therefore, the activity should not be described as the strongest translanguaging case in the corpus. It is more accurate to say that it integrates translanguaging as a bridge within an otherwise very strong SIOP and 5E lesson design.

Activity excerpt: The assessment section mainly says, "Teacher observes and notes student participation and accuracy" and "Anecdotal notes on language and content understanding." It does not explicitly require assessment of bilingual products as bilingual products.

## State Standards Evidence

Activity 13 includes explicit state standards alignment. The main activity cites California NGSS K-PS2-1 and CA CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.SL.K.1. The extension activity cites California NGSS K-PS2-2 and CA CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.SL.K.4. This standards section strengthens the case because the activity is not only pedagogically structured; it is also tied to grade-level science and language expectations.

Activity excerpt: "California Science Standard (NGSS): K-PS2-1: Plan and conduct an investigation to compare the effects of different strengths or different directions of pushes and pulls on the motion of an object."

Activity excerpt: "California ELA Standard: CA CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.SL.K.1: Participate in collaborative conversations with diverse partners about kindergarten topics and texts with peers and adults in small and larger groups."

## Paste-Ready Interpretation

Activity 13 was selected as the representative SIOP case because it provided the strongest and most comprehensive written evidence of SIOP alignment across the 35 SciLing activities. The activity, a kindergarten physical science lesson on comparing pushes and pulls, received a SIOP average of 3.73 out of 4, with 29 of 30 SIOP features scored as clearly or highly evident and all eight SIOP components meeting the high-alignment threshold. Its strength lies in the way it integrates content and language objectives, hands-on materials, clear task sequencing, proficiency-level scaffolds, structured interaction, review, assessment, and California standards alignment into a coherent activity.

As a qualitative sample, Activity 13 illustrates how SciLing can generate a lesson design that reflects the SIOP model as a whole rather than isolated language supports. Students explore familiar objects, use gestures and visuals, participate in mixed-proficiency group discussion, apply sentence frames, and show understanding through oral, visual, and written/drawn responses. The activity also maps strongly onto the 5E model: students are engaged through familiar classroom objects, explore pushes and pulls through manipulation, explain findings through discussion and charting, elaborate through an extension challenge, and evaluate understanding through quick checks and teacher notes. Translanguaging is present as a bridge for multilingual learners: students may discuss findings in their home language, share home-language terms for key concepts, use bilingual labels or picture cards, and label drawings in English and/or their home language. Thus, Activity 13 is an appropriate representative case for showing how SIOP, inquiry-based 5E design, translanguaging supports, and standards alignment can be integrated in a single SciLing-generated activity, while also noting that its translanguaging evidence is moderate rather than maximal.

## Caution for Reporting

The analysis should state that scores are based only on Column C written activity text. Low or missing evidence for features such as wait time, feedback, or flexible multilingual assessment does not prove that these practices would be absent in classroom implementation. It means only that the written activity did not explicitly document them.
